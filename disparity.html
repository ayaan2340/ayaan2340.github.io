Race Disparities in Prison

Written by Ayaan Sunesara

Through "colorblind" drug policies and the War on Drugs, the criminal justice system has been used to target African Americans. The effects of these policies can be seen in the racial disparities in the prison population. African Americans make up 13% of the US population, but they make up nearly 33% of the state prison population. Since being in prison and having a criminal record can limit job opportunities, housing opportunities, and voting rights, a disproportionate number of African Americans are at a disadvantage in society.

For example, in The New Jim Crow , Michelle Alexander writes, "To put the matter starkly: the current system of control permanently locks a huge percentage of the African American community out of the mainstream society and economy. The system operates through our criminal justice institutions, but it functions more like a caste system than a system of crime control."

The most blatant example of a colorblind drug policy that aims to put more black people in jail is the crack vs. cocaine sentencing disparity. Crack and cocaine are the same drug, but crack is a cheaper form of cocaine that is more commonly used by African Americans. The Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986 established a 100:1 sentencing disparity between crack and cocaine. This means that someone who is caught with 5 grams of crack would receive the same sentence as someone who is caught with 500 grams of cocaine. This disparity was reduced to 18:1 in 2010, but it still exists today.
