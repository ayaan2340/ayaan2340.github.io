Drug Crime

Written by Ayaan Sunesara

Simple crimes such as drug possession and drug use have been the main reason for the increase in the prison population. People convicted of drug crimes began to be labeled as felons despite the fact that they were not violent offenders. If the War on Drugs was truly about stopping drug use, then resources would be dedicated to treatment and rehabilitation. However, the War on Drugs has been used to target African Americans and other minorities who are in a vulnerable position. The methods by which the criminal justice system receives a disproportionate amount of African Americans each year includes racial profiling, over-policing, mandatory minimums, and the criminalization of drug use.

The introduction of federal mandatory minimum sentences exemplifies how the punishment for drug crimes was intensified during the War on Drugs. These laws have resulted in individuals, often disproportionately Black and Hispanic, receiving lengthy prison sentences regardless of the circumstances surrounding their offenses. Such rigid sentencing requirements have contributed to over-incarceration, strained the criminal justice system, and exacerbated racial disparities in arrests and convictions. Mandatory minimums have also hindered judges' ability to consider individual circumstances and appropriate punishments, leading to cases of disproportionate sentencing and limiting the potential for rehabilitation and reintegration into society. Often, these sentences are longer than necessary to achieve the goals of sentencing, which include punishment, deterrence, and rehabilitation.

Being released from prison is not the end of the punishment for drug crimes. Once released, individuals are often denied access to public housing, student loans, and employment opportunities. The label of a "felon" is a permanent label that can be used to discriminate against individuals for the rest of their lives. Not only are they barred legally from reentering society, but the stigma of being a felon can also prevent them from being accepted by their communities. All of these lifetime consequences can be the result of a simple drug possession charge.

"If we hope to end this system of control, we cannot be satisfied with a handful of reforms. All of the financial incentives granted to law enforcement to arrest poor black and brown people for drug offenses must be revoked...Black and brown people in ghetto communities must no longer be viewed as the designated enemy, and ghetto communities must no longer be treated like occupied zones. A compassionate, humane approach to the problems of the urban poor must replace the punitive practices and policies that have multiplied the harms associated with poverty and racial oppression." - Michelle Alexander, The New Jim Crow
