Growing Prison Populations

Written by Ayaan Sunesara

Since the 1980s, the prison population of America has been consistently growing at an alarming rate due to new criminial justice policies and the war on drugs. Even though crime rates in the US have been decreasing since the 1990s, the prison population has continued to increase. This contradiction is not the result of an accident, but it is the result of colorblind policies that have been implemented in the criminal justice system. These policies have been implemented in a way that has disproportionately affected African Americans, and trapped them into a racial caste system which prevents progress in the black community.

In the book, The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness Michelle Alexander writes about how the criminal justice system has been used as a tool to oppress African Americans. She writes about how the criminal justice system has been used to create a racial caste system that has been used to discriminate against African Americans. The release of this book in 2010 sparked a new social movement to reform the criminal justice system, and it has been a major influence in the creation of this website.

As shown by the graph, the correctional population is around 600% larger than the prison population. Not only does the criminal justice system affect those who are in prison, but it also affects those who are on parole, probation, or in jail. The correctional population is made up of all of these groups, and it is important to understand that the criminal justice system affects more than just those who are in prison. Currently, the correctional population can be legally discriminated against in jobs, housing, and voting rights.

Michelle Alexander writes in her book The New Jim Crow , "The United States has a staggering 2.3 million people in prison-a higher rate of incarceration than any other country in the world-but it also has another 4.5 million people on probation or parole. More than 70 million Americans-over 20 percent of the entire U.S population, overwhelmingly poor and disproportionately people of color-now have criminal records that authorize legal discrimination for life."
